# The Bloomsbury Group

## Origins

After the death of their parents, **Virginia** and her siblings **Vanessa**, **Thoby**, and **Adrian** moved in **1905** to the **Bloomsbury district of London**. This move was a symbolic break from the rigid Victorian world of Kensington where they had grown up. Their new home in **Gordon Square**, and later in **Tavistock Square**, became the meeting place of a group of young intellectuals who rejected Victorian conventions and embraced freedom of thought, artistic experimentation, and unconventional relationships.

## The Circle

This group became known as the **Bloomsbury Group**, and it included painters, writers, economists, and critics. Its main members were:

- **Roger Fry** — painter and art critic
- **Vanessa Bell** — painter and Virginia Woolf's sister
- **Duncan Grant** — painter and designer
- **Lytton Strachey** — biographer
- **E.M. Forster** — novelist
- **John Maynard Keynes** — economist
- **Leonard Woolf** — writer and Virginia's husband

## Ideas and Influence

The group was deeply influenced by **Roger Fry's 1910 Post-Impressionist Exhibition**, which introduced the work of **Cézanne, Matisse, Gauguin, Picasso, and Van Gogh** to British audiences. This was a turning point in British culture. Bloomsbury members began experimenting with new artistic ideas — exploring **colour, form, and abstraction** through the **Omega Workshops**, where Duncan Grant became a leading figure.

Bloomsbury was active not only in painting but also in design, through open discussion, and in publishing through the **Hogarth Press** (founded by Leonard and Virginia Woolf). The group's lifestyle and ideas deeply shaped **Virginia Woolf's writing** and her artistic innovations.

## Values and Legacy

The Bloomsbury Group stood for **sexual freedom**, the **rejection of Victorian morality**, and the belief that art and ideas should be free from the rules of religion, nation, and family. They challenged conventional society and helped shape modern British culture in literature, art, and thought.

Their values connect directly to **James Joyce's modernist ideal**: in *A Portrait of the Artist as a Young Man*, Joyce's alter ego Stephen Dedalus declares his refusal to serve church, nation, or family — choosing instead **"silence, exile, and cunning"** to create new forms of expression. This reflects the broader modernist belief in breaking away from inherited traditions.
